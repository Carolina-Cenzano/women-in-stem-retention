Project 1 · Survey analysis

Do women who study STEM stay in STEM?

The United States graduates hundreds of thousands of women in science and engineering, yet the workforce tells a different story. This study follows women who earned a bachelor's degree in computer science, mathematics, physics, chemistry or engineering between 2005 and 2019, and asks how many are actually practicing today, whether pay gaps push them out, and when the leaving happens.

The data

Everything on this page comes from one source: the 2023 National Survey of College Graduates public use file, a survey run by the US National Science Foundation with about 94,000 respondents weighted to represent the college-educated population. No synthetic data, no scraped estimates.

The study population is everyone whose first bachelor's degree was earned at a US institution between 2005 and 2019, in one of the five majors, split into three five-year cohorts (2005–2010, 2010–2015, 2015–2020, half-open so no year is counted twice). "Practicing STEM" means the survey classified the person's principal job as a science and engineering occupation. That leaves 13,022 respondents in the analysis sample, 3,413 of them women, and every figure below uses the survey weights, so percentages describe the real US population, not just the sample.

The headline

Of the roughly 870,000 US women who earned a bachelor's degree in these five majors between 2005 and 2019, well under half work in a science and engineering occupation today. The gap to men with the same degrees is about sixteen percentage points, and it exists in every field except the smallest ones where the estimates get noisy.

Retention by field and cohort

Does the pay gap drive women out?

A reasonable hypothesis: fields where women earn visibly less than men should lose more women. The survey lets us test the association directly, since it records salaries for everyone working in an S&E occupation.

The answer, in this data, is no: the correlation is weak and statistically indistinguishable from zero. Mathematics has the highest female attrition and a modest pay gap; chemistry pays women better than men and still loses two thirds of them. Whatever pushes women out of these fields, a simple salary differential does not explain it.

When does the leaving happen?

A single survey cannot follow individuals over decades, but it can do the next best thing: compare otherwise similar graduates at different distances from their degree. If women left STEM steadily over time, the share still practicing would fall as years since graduation increase.

This is the study's clearest structural finding: a large share of women who studied these fields never practice them at all. Attrition after entry exists, but it is modest next to the gap at the start of the career. Retention programs aimed at mid-career women address the smaller half of the problem in this population.

Honest limitations

  • This is a cross-section, not a panel. The survey photographs 2023; nobody is tracked over time. The years-since-degree chart compares different people at different career stages, so cohort effects and period effects (for instance, the tech hiring cycle) are entangled with age effects.
  • Association is not causation. The pay-gap analysis is a correlation across five fields. Five points cannot support a causal claim in either direction, which is why the survey's own stated-reasons question is shown alongside it.
  • Small cells are flagged, not hidden. Women physics graduates number 124 in the sample; estimates involving them carry wide uncertainty and are greyed wherever the cell drops below 30 respondents.
  • Definitions matter. "Practicing STEM" here means an S&E occupation under the NCSES classification. Counting S&E-related occupations (health, S&E management, technical teaching) as practicing would raise every retention number substantially.

Reproduce it

The full pipeline is in this repository: scripts/download_data.py fetches the survey file from the NSF, scripts/prepare_data.py builds every aggregate on this page, and the dashboard is plain HTML plus Plotly. The raw microdata is not committed (it is 140 MB), but one script call restores it. There is a full walkthrough of the data preparation in the data-preparation walkthrough.
